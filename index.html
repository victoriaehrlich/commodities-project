About the next few weeks

I'm coming to the end of my mentorship, and rather than just working on small projects, we decided to do something a bit more deliberate: a four-week collaboration project. A shared GitHub repository, clear goals, and a proper introduction to what it actually means to work on code with someone else.

That last part - GitHub - is essential when collaborating on projects. Version control is one of those things that sounds straightforward until you're actually in it: understanding branches, commits, when to push and when to pull, and why it matters when two people are touching the same files. It's less glamorous than building a chart, but it's the kind of infrastructure that makes collaborative work actually function.

So this post is going to work a little differently. Instead of a fresh article each week, I'll be updating this one - adding to it as I work through what I want to learn over the next few weeks. The plan so far: making the charts interactive so you can hover over data points, adding the ability to toggle between fertiliser prices and crude oil, building a bar chart with a new dataset, and - thanks to my mentor and collaborator - we're pushing a map in too. Genuinely excited about that last one and learning from him whilst collaborating on a topic and an industry we're both interested in: data journalism.

Alongside all of this, I'm still working through the D3 book chapter by chapter. There's something satisfying about having a goal-shaped project to apply the theory to as you go — it stops the reading from feeling abstract.

Come back and see what's been added. Have a nice weekend!

The Cost of Fertiliser

Global fertiliser prices are rising again. Here's why it matters.

Global fertiliser prices have begun rising again as conflict involving Iran threatens production and exports from the Middle East. It echoes what happened after Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022, when fertiliser and energy markets were thrown into turmoil. But this time the risk is different: the Middle East sits at the heart of global fertiliser supply.

The region is both a major producer and a crucial transit hub. Around 35% of globally traded urea passes through the Strait of Hormuz, one of the world’s most important shipping routes. Urea is the most widely used nitrogen fertiliser and is what keeps a huge share of the world's crops growing. Any disruption to production in the Gulf - or to shipping through the strait - can quickly tighten supply for farmers around the world.

The timing feels familiar. Russia's invasion of Ukraine began in February 2022, just as farmers across the northern hemisphere were preparing for the planting season. The same is happening now. As farmers start buying fertiliser for the coming season, rising prices and potential shortages could start affecting what they actually decide to plant.

But the impact might play out differently this time. Ukraine's invasion immediately disrupted grain exports, sending food prices sharply higher. Fertiliser shocks work more slowly: they raise the cost of growing crops rather than removing food from markets overnight. But if farmers can't afford fertiliser or can't access it, yields could fall across multiple regions later in the year.
