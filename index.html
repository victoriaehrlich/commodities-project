The Cost of Fertiliser and Fuel

Two commodities. One crisis. Here's why both matter.

Global fertiliser and crude oil prices have both begun rising again as conflict involving Iran threatens production and exports from the Middle East. It echoes what happened after Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022, when energy and agricultural markets were thrown into turmoil together. That connection is not a coincidence - the two commodities are deeply linked.

Crude oil drives the cost of natural gas, and natural gas is the primary feedstock for producing urea, the world's most widely used nitrogen fertiliser. When oil prices rise, fertiliser production becomes more expensive almost immediately. The Middle East sits at the centre of both supply chains: it is a major crude oil exporter and a key producer and transit point for fertilisers, with around 35% of globally traded urea passing through the Strait of Hormuz. A disruption to one market rarely stays contained to one market.

The timing feels familiar. Russia's invasion of Ukraine in February 2022 triggered simultaneous shocks across both markets - oil prices spiked, gas supplies tightened, and fertiliser costs surged just as farmers across the northern hemisphere were preparing for the planting season. The same pressure is building now, and with it the same downstream risk: if fertiliser becomes too expensive or too scarce, farmers adjust what they plant and how much - and that ripples through food supply months later.

But the two commodities transmit their shocks differently. An oil price spike is felt quickly - in transport, energy bills, and production costs within weeks. Fertiliser works on a slower clock. Farmers commit to their inputs before the growing season begins, so a price surge in spring can quietly reduce yields by autumn.

The countries most vulnerable to these swings are those whose entire economies are built on oil revenues. When prices spike or supply is disrupted, the shock is not just felt at the petrol pump - it ripples through national budgets, public spending, and political stability. And several of those countries sit in the same region that is now at the centre of the current crisis.

The immediate question is whether the current ceasefire holds - and what follows if it does. Brent crude has steadied around $94 per barrel, with markets weighing the prospect of a broader agreement that could reopen the Strait of Hormuz against the reality that the waterway remains effectively closed. Analysts expect any supply recovery to be gradual, given the logistical constraints involved. That matters for both commodities in this story: a sustained easing in oil prices would, over time, feed through to lower gas costs and ease the pressure on fertiliser production. But the calendar is working against farmers who need to make planting decisions now, not once diplomacy has run its course. The harvest that follows will reflect choices already being made - under prices that have not yet come down.
